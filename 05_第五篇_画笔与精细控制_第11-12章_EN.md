### Chapter 11: Opacity vs. Flow — The Ultimate Guide

In Photoshop's vast toolbox, few parameters are as fundamental — and as easily misunderstood — as Opacity and Flow.

To beginners, they both seem like sliders that simply "lighten" or "fade" the brush color. On the surface, their functions appear similar — both make brush strokes lighter. However, their underlying mechanics are completely different, and using them incorrectly causes a variety of problems.

A deep understanding and flexible application of the difference between Opacity and Flow is the dividing line between a Photoshop "user" and a Photoshop "master." It directly affects the subtlety, realism, and professional quality of your work. Whether it's color transitions in digital painting, light-and-shadow sculpting in portrait retouching, or seamless masks in creative composites, everything hinges on precise control of these two parameters.

**Chapter learning objectives**:

1. **Differentiate between Layer-level Opacity and Fill**, and master their application in advanced layer style design.

2. **Understand the fundamental mechanics of Opacity vs. Flow in the Brush Tool**. This is the core content of the chapter, explaining in detail how the two differ.

3. **Learn how to combine a pressure-sensitive stylus with Opacity and Flow** to create expressive brush strokes. The stylus is the core device for digital painting, and mastering it significantly enhances the creative experience.

4. **Apply theory to practice**, learning the optimal settings strategy for professional scenarios such as digital painting, photo retouching, and mask work. Theory must ultimately serve practice.

### 11.1 Foundation — Opacity vs. Fill in the Layers Panel

Before discussing brushes, we must first resolve a source of confusion. **Flow is a property specific to brush-type tools. In the Layers Panel, the counterpart to Opacity is Fill.**

In most cases, these two appear to produce identical results, but one key distinction makes Fill a powerful tool for advanced design techniques.

Many beginners confuse Opacity and Fill because they look so similar. In reality, their scopes of effect are completely different — which produces dramatically different results.

#### 11.1.1 What Is Layer Opacity?

**Definition**: Layer Opacity controls the visibility of the **entire layer**.

**Scope of effect**: It uniformly affects **everything** on that layer, including:

- The layer's own pixels (images, shapes, fills, etc.).
- All **Layer Styles** applied to the layer, such as Drop Shadow, Stroke, Inner Glow, etc.

When you reduce a layer's Opacity from 100%, imagine placing a semi-transparent sheet of glass over the entire layer — together with all its decorative effects. The lower the number, the more opaque the glass, until at 0% it becomes completely invisible.

This means: Opacity affects everything on the layer — both the layer content and the layer styles. For example, if you reduce the Opacity of a text layer with a Drop Shadow to 50%, both the text and the shadow fade together.

#### 11.1.2 What Is Layer Fill?

**Definition**: Layer Fill controls the visibility of the **layer's original content**.

**Scope of effect**: It **only affects** the parts of the layer composed directly of pixels, and **does not affect** the **Layer Styles** attached to that layer.

This is the most critical difference. When you lower Fill, the Layer Styles (such as Drop Shadow, Stroke, etc.) retain their original opacity; only the layer's own content fades.

This means: Fill only affects the "substance," not the "effects." For example, if you reduce the Fill of a text layer that has a red Stroke and a Drop Shadow to 0%, the text itself disappears (0% opacity), but the red Stroke and Drop Shadow remain perfectly intact!

#### 11.1.3 Comparison Summary & Practice

| Feature | Layer Opacity | Layer Fill |
|---|---|---|
| **Controls** | Entire layer (content + styles) | Layer content only |
| **Effect on Layer Styles** | Affects them | No effect |
| **Core use** | Adjust overall layer visibility for blending/overlay | Create special effects where content is transparent but styles remain visible |

#### Teaching Experiment: Creating a "Ghost Text" Effect

This classic example most intuitively demonstrates Fill's unique role.

1. **Create text**: Create a new document in Photoshop, use the Type Tool (T) to enter any word, e.g., "GHOST." A bold font is recommended, with black as the color.

    First open Photoshop, press Ctrl+N to create a new document, then select the Type Tool and enter the word "GHOST." A bolder font makes the effect more apparent, and black is the best color choice.

2. **Add Layer Styles**: Double-click the text layer to open the Layer Style dialog.

    Double-click the layer to open the Layer Style dialog, or select the layer and go to Menu > Layer > Layer Style.

    - Check and enter **Stroke**. Set `Size` to 10 px, `Position` to `Outside`, and `Color` to a bright red.

        Stroke adds an outline around the text edge. Choosing bright red creates a strong contrast with the black text.

    - Check and enter **Drop Shadow**. Keep the default settings.

        Drop Shadow adds a shadow behind the text, enhancing the three-dimensional feel. Default settings usually produce a good result.

    - Click "OK." You should now see black text with a red Stroke and a gray Drop Shadow.

3. **Test Opacity**: In the Layers Panel, select the text layer and gradually drag the **Opacity** slider from 100% down to 0%. You will observe that the text, Stroke, and Drop Shadow — as a unified whole — fade together until completely gone. Then restore Opacity to 100%.

    Opacity affects everything — text, Stroke, and Drop Shadow all fade simultaneously. At 0% Opacity, nothing is visible.

4. **Test Fill**: Now, keep Opacity at 100% and gradually drag the **Fill** slider from 100% down to 0%.

    - **A magical phenomenon occurs**: The black text itself gradually disappears, but the red Stroke around it and the Drop Shadow below remain perfectly intact!

    This is Fill's unique property: it hides only the "substance" while preserving the "effects."

> **Professional application scenarios**:
> The "content transparent, styles preserved" effect is very popular in UI design, logo design, and creative posters. It can be used to create glass-texture buttons, hollow glowing text, watermark effects, and more. Remember this rule: **When you want to preserve a layer's effects (light, shadow, outline) but hide its substance, use Fill.**

### 11.2 Core Concept — Opacity vs. Flow in Brushes

Now we arrive at the heart of this chapter. In the Brush Tool (and other similar tools like the Eraser, Clone Stamp, Dodge/Burn tools), the Options Bar displays both Opacity and Flow.

This is where confusion most commonly arises. Both Opacity and Flow are parameters that control how "light" a brush stroke is, but they work in fundamentally different ways — using the wrong one leads to various problems.

#### 11.2.1 What Is Brush Opacity?

**Core mechanism: Per-stroke opacity ceiling.**

Think of Opacity as a painter who is instructed: "No matter how you paint this wall, the final transparency must not exceed 20%."

- **Single stroke**: In Photoshop, a single stroke is the entire process from the moment you **press down the mouse button (or touch down with the stylus)** until you **release the mouse button (or lift the stylus)**.

    This is a complete motion from press to release. During this time, you may move the brush across the canvas drawing lines, but the entire process counts as one "stroke."

- **Ceiling effect**: As long as you stay within the **same stroke**, even if you repeatedly brush over the same area, the opacity of the color will **never exceed** the Opacity value you set.

    No matter how many times you brush over the same spot — drawing a line, going back and forth, repeatedly — the deepest color you can achieve will never exceed the Opacity ceiling. For example, if Opacity is set to 20%, the final result will only ever be 20% gray, no matter how many times you paint over it.

- **Building up**: To make the color darker, you must **lift the brush and start a new stroke**, then paint over the same area again. Each new stroke layers on top of the previous one using multiplicative blending, making the color progressively darker.

    If you want a deeper color, you must start a new stroke. The second stroke builds on the first, the third on the first two... It's like painting a wall — each coat applies a thin layer, and you must wait for one coat to dry before applying the next.

**Feel**: Opacity feels more "digital" and "absolute." It provides a predictable, precise result. The result of each stroke is expected, and it does not change based on movement speed or repeated brushing.

#### 11.2.2 What Is Brush Flow?

**Core mechanism: Rate of paint application.**

Think of Flow as the valve on a spray can or the water content of a watercolor brush. Flow controls the speed and density at which paint is "sprayed" onto the canvas as you move the brush.

- **Build-up effect**: Within the **same stroke**, as long as you keep the brush button held down and repeatedly brush over the same area, the color will **continuously build up**, getting darker with each pass, exactly like holding a spray can over one spot. This accumulation continues until it reaches 100% opacity (solid color).

    This is Flow's unique property: it allows continuous darkening within a single stroke. With Flow at 20%, the first pass gives 20% gray, the second overlay gives 40% gray, the third gives 60% gray... As long as you keep brushing the same area, the color keeps getting darker.

- **Effect of movement speed**: Flow's result is also affected by how fast you move the brush. Moving quickly means less "paint" per unit area, resulting in a lighter color; moving slowly gives a darker color.

    This simulates the behavior of real painting tools. When moving the brush quickly, the paint doesn't have enough time to deposit; when moving slowly, more paint adheres to the surface.

**Feel**: Flow feels more "analog" and "organic." It mimics the process in traditional painting (like airbrushing or pencil sketching) where tones and shadows are gradually built up through repeated application. This cumulative property makes transitions smoother and more natural.

#### 11.2.3 The Ultimate Comparison Experiment

Prepare to experience the difference firsthand. This experiment will fundamentally change your understanding of these two parameters.

1. **Preparation**:

    - Create a new document (Ctrl+N / Cmd+N).

    - Select the Brush Tool (B). Press B to select the Brush Tool.

    - Choose a standard **soft round brush**. Select Soft Round from the Brush Presets. Soft-edged brushes have a gradual edge that produces soft transitions.

    - Set the foreground color to pure black (#000000). Press D to reset foreground/background to default, then press X to switch the foreground to black.

    - Adjust the brush size to a suitable value (e.g., 200 px). Press the left bracket [ and right bracket ] to quickly adjust brush size.

2. **Experiment 1: Low Opacity vs. Low Flow**

    - **Scenario A: Low Opacity, High Flow**

        - **Settings**: Opacity: 20%, Flow: 100%

            Set these two values in the Options Bar.

        - **Action**: Hold down the mouse button without releasing, and quickly draw a back-and-forth "Z" shape on the canvas.

            Keep the button held down without releasing, and draw a Z.

        - **Observation**: You will get a uniform, 20% gray "Z." No matter how many times the strokes overlap within the same uninterrupted stroke, the color does not darken. Now, lift the brush and draw another "Z" over the first one. You will notice that the overlapping area becomes darker.

            This is Opacity's "ceiling effect" — within the same stroke, the color never exceeds 20%. Only by starting a new stroke does the color build up.

        - **Conclusion**: Opacity sets a strict "ceiling" for each single stroke.

    - **Scenario B: High Opacity, Low Flow**

        - **Settings**: Opacity: 100%, Flow: 20%

            Set these values in the Options Bar.

        - **Action**: Again, hold down the mouse button without releasing, and quickly draw a back-and-forth "Z" shape on the canvas.

        - **Observation**: Witness the magic! Although each individual pass leaves a light mark, the color **immediately starts building up** where strokes overlap, becoming darker. At the corners of the "Z," where the brush passed the most times, the color is noticeably darker than elsewhere. If you repeatedly circle around one spot, it quickly becomes pure black.

            This is Flow's "build-up effect" — as long as the stroke is unbroken, the color keeps getting darker.

        - **Conclusion**: Flow allows unlimited color build-up within a single stroke, all the way to 100%.

3. **Experiment 2: Exploring Combined Effects**

    - **Scenario C: Low Opacity, Low Flow**

        - **Settings**: Opacity: 50%, Flow: 20%

        - **Action**: Draw another "Z."

        - **Observation**: This is a combined effect. Flow allows the color to build up when strokes overlap, but Opacity sets the **maximum opacity ceiling** for this single stroke: no matter how much you build up, the final result cannot exceed 50%. In other words, you can repeatedly circle around one spot, and the color will darken from light gray, but it will stop at 50% gray — it cannot go deeper. Unless you lift the brush and start a new stroke.

    This demonstrates the effect of combining both parameters: Opacity sets the ceiling, Flow controls the rate of accumulation.

#### 11.2.4 Comparison Summary

| Feature | Brush Opacity | Brush Flow |
|---|---|---|
| **Core metaphor** | Opacity **ceiling** | Paint **flow rate** |
| **Build-up within a single stroke** | **No** — color never exceeds the set value | **Yes** — color continuously builds up |
| **Feel** | Digital, precise, predictable | Analog, organic, ideal for gradients |
| **Best suited for** | Flat areas needing uniform opacity, graphic design | Digital painting, smooth transitions, mask refinement |

### 11.3 The Path to Mastery — Advanced Techniques & Stylus Settings

#### 11.3.1 Combining with a Stylus: Unlocking True Power

For users of Wacom and other pressure-sensitive styluses, control of Opacity and Flow enters an entirely new dimension.

The stylus is the core input device for digital painting. Unlike a regular mouse, a stylus senses the pressure of your strokes — a light touch produces fine lines, harder pressure produces thicker lines. This pressure sensitivity can be mapped to various brush parameters, bringing the painting experience closer to traditional media.

- **Open the Brush Settings Panel**: Press `F5`.

    F5 is the shortcut for Brush Settings. This panel is one of Photoshop's most powerful customization tools.

- **Shape Dynamics**: Here, set the `Control` for `Size Jitter` to `Pen Pressure`. This is the most basic setting, making the thickness of strokes vary with pressure.

    With this, heavier pressure produces thicker lines, lighter pressure produces thinner lines — simulating the behavior of real brushes.

- **Transfer** (in older versions of Photoshop) or **Opacity & Flow Jitter** (in newer versions of PS Brush Settings):

    - **Opacity Jitter**: Set its `Control` to `Pen Pressure`. This means the **pressure** of your stroke directly determines the Opacity value. Light pressure gives low Opacity, heavy pressure gives high Opacity, but it still follows Opacity's "single-stroke ceiling" principle.

    - **Flow Jitter**: Set its `Control` to `Pen Pressure`. This means your **pressure** determines the "flow rate" of paint. This is the core setting for the vast majority of digital painters and retouchers. A light touch gives very low Flow, suitable for fine transitions; heavy pressure gives high Flow, allowing rapid color application. This approach best replicates the experience of real painting.

> **Professional advice**:
> For painting and retouching, it is recommended to **turn off pressure sensitivity for Opacity** (set Opacity Jitter to 0% or Off), and **enable pressure sensitivity for Flow** (set Flow Jitter control to Pen Pressure). At the same time, set Opacity to 100% in the Options Bar and Flow to a low initial value (e.g., 20–30%). This way, you can completely control the rate of color build-up through the weight of your strokes, without being interfered with by Opacity's "ceiling" — giving you the smoothest, most intuitive painting experience.

This is the standard setup for professional digital artists: Opacity pressure sensitivity off, Flow pressure sensitivity on, Opacity at 100%, Flow at 20–30%.

#### 11.3.2 Airbrush Mode

In the Options Bar, next to the Flow slider, there is an airbrush icon.

- **Function**: When Airbrush mode is enabled, Flow behaves more like a real airbrush. Even when your **brush is stationary**, as long as you hold down the mouse button (or keep the stylus in contact with the tablet), paint will continuously "spray" and build up.

    In normal mode, paint deposition requires brush movement — no movement, no new paint. But in Airbrush mode, even a stationary brush keeps spraying.

- **Difference**: In normal mode, Flow build-up requires brush **movement**. In Airbrush mode, a **stationary** brush also builds up.

- **Use**: Ideal for creating spray effects, granular textures, or for gentle, sustained color accumulation at specific points.

### 11.4 Applying Knowledge — Use in Professional Workflows

The ultimate purpose of theory is to serve practice. Below are strategic approaches to using Opacity and Flow in several common professional scenarios.

#### 11.4.1 Digital Painting & Illustration

- **Recommended settings**: Opacity: `100%`, Flow: `5–30%`, Flow Jitter (Pen Pressure): `On`.

    Keep Opacity at 100% so color can build up. Set Flow to a low initial value and enable pressure sensitivity to control Flow.

- **Workflow**:

    1. **Laying down base colors**: Use a higher Flow (50%+) to quickly block in base colors.

        Blocking in large areas doesn't require fine control; higher Flow covers larger areas quickly.

    2. **Creating transitions**: Use a low Flow (10–20%) at the border between two colors, gently brushing back and forth with the stylus. Flow's cumulative property creates extremely smooth, natural color transitions — something Opacity struggles to achieve.

        Color transitions are one of the hardest aspects of digital painting. Flow's cumulative property makes transitions natural — light back-and-forth brushing gradually deepens the color, creating a soft, organic blend.

    3. **Sculpting volume**: When painting a sphere or a face, repeatedly and gently brush with a low-Flow brush over lit and shadowed areas, gradually "building up" a three-dimensional form — just like shading with a pencil.

        The principle of pencil shading: layers upon layers of graphite build different depths of gray. Flow's cumulative property perfectly simulates this process.

#### 11.4.2 High-End Portrait Retouching: Dodge & Burn

This is a core technique that demonstrates a retoucher's skill, relying entirely on masterful control of Flow.

- **Recommended settings**: Opacity: `100%`, Flow: `1–3%`.

    Extremely low Flow is key — this enables incredibly fine control.

- **Workflow (50% Gray Layer Method)**:

    1. Create a new layer and fill it with 50% gray (RGB: 128, 128, 128).

        Press Ctrl+Shift+N to create a new layer, then use Shift+F5 to fill, selecting Gray.

    2. Set the new layer's Blending Mode to **Overlay**.

        In Overlay mode, 50% gray is completely neutral and does not affect the underlying image.

    3. Select the Brush Tool, set Flow to 1–3%.

        The extremely low Flow value allows you to control with surgical precision.

    4. **Dodge**: Set the foreground color to **white**. On the gray layer, gently brush over areas of the face that need brightening (e.g., the bridge of the nose, cheekbone highlights). Because Flow is extremely low, each stroke produces only an imperceptible brightening. You need to brush repeatedly to "sculpt" the light, resulting in an exceptionally delicate effect with no harsh color patches.

        Extremely low Flow lets you sculpt light like a sculptor working clay. Each stroke makes a minuscule change, and the accumulation produces perfect lighting.

    5. **Burn**: Set the foreground color to **black**. Brush over areas that need darkening (e.g., the sides of the cheeks, nostrils).

        Again, the extremely low Flow produces an exquisitely subtle result.

    - **Why use Flow?**: Imagine using 5% Opacity — each stroke would be a fixed 5% gray block, and multiple clicks would produce hard edges. In contrast, 1% Flow allows you to build up smoothly within an area from a 0.1% change to 5%, with incomparably greater control.

    This is why professional retouchers use Flow instead of Opacity: Flow creates perfectly smooth transitions, while Opacity produces noticeable patch edges.

#### 11.4.3 The Art of Masking

Masks control the visible area of a layer and are critical to compositing. The more natural the black-to-white transition in a mask, the more realistic the composite.

- **Recommended settings**: Opacity: `100%`, Flow: `10–50%`.

- **Workflow**:

    1. Add a mask to the layer.

        Select the layer and click the "Add Layer Mask" button at the bottom of the Layers Panel.

    2. Select the Brush Tool and paint with black on the mask to hide parts of the layer, or white to reveal them.

        On a mask: Black = hide, White = reveal.

    3. **Handling hard edges**: For complex edges like hair strands or leaves, use a low Flow (10–20%) brush to process the edges more precisely, making the transition look less like a "cutout."

        Low Flow allows you to paint delicately along edges, creating smooth semi-transparent transitions.

    4. **Creating gradients**: To make an object (e.g., a sky) blend smoothly into a background, use a large soft round brush on the mask with a low Flow, sweeping from the area that should be 100% visible toward the area that should be hidden. Flow's cumulative effect automatically creates a perfect gradient.

        Gradients are one of the most common mask uses. Flow makes gradient creation natural — a quick sweep creates a rapid transition, a slow brush creates a gradual one.

### 11.5 Conclusion: Your New Creative Philosophy

By now, you should deeply understand that Opacity and Flow are far more than simple sliders — they represent two fundamentally different creative philosophies.

- **Opacity is an architect**: Precise, planned, establishing boundaries that cannot be crossed. Use it when you need clear, controllable results. For example, when a certain element in your image must remain at 50% opacity — use Opacity.

- **Flow is a sculptor**: Organic, intuitive, shaping form through layers of accumulation. Use it when you need delicate, natural transitions and artistic expression. For example, when you need to create a smooth color blend — use Flow.

**Final advice**:
In your next creative session, try **locking brush Opacity at 100%** and adjusting only **Flow** and **stylus pressure**. It may feel awkward at first, but once you master the rhythm of controlling your image through Flow's accumulation, your Photoshop skills will enter an entirely new realm. Forget about harsh strokes with visible edges; embrace colors that blend as naturally as real paint.

### Chapter 12: Why Use 100% Opacity + Low Flow in Masks?

Let's first review the nature of masks: On a mask, pure black represents **completely hidden** (0% visible), pure white represents **completely visible** (100% visible), and gray represents varying degrees of **semi-transparency**. Our goal is to create smooth, natural gray transitions.

A mask is a tool that controls layer visibility. White areas fully reveal the layer content, black areas fully hide it, and gray produces a semi-transparent effect.

#### 12.1 Avoiding the "Stamping Effect"

- **The problem with low Opacity**:
    Suppose you set the brush `Opacity` to 20% and `Flow` to 100%. You click once, and it stamps a 20% gray "stamp" on the mask. You click again, and where these two "stamps" overlap, the color is multiplicatively blended, becoming darker than the surrounding area and creating an ugly, hard edge. Even with continuous brushing, every lift and press of the brush produces these unnatural overlapping marks.

    This is the "stamping effect" — Opacity creates sharp boundaries between strokes, looking as unnatural as a rubber stamp.

- **The advantage of low Flow**:
    Now, set the brush `Opacity` to 100% and `Flow` to 20%. Hold the brush down without releasing and paint on the mask. The paint will **gradually build up** like an airbrush. As long as your stroke is unbroken, no harsh edges appear. You can brush repeatedly over the same area until you reach the desired gray depth (ultimately pure black or pure white), and the entire process is incredibly smooth and organic.

    Flow's cumulative nature perfectly avoids this problem — no stroke boundaries, only smooth transitions.

#### 12.2 Achieving Extremely Smooth Transitions

This is the most important reason. Whether extracting a portrait from a background or compositing a new sky into a landscape photo, the key is natural edge transitions.

- Low `Flow` allows you to "build up" the mask edge layer by layer, gently and finely, just like shading the dark areas of a pencil drawing. Starting with `Flow: 5%`, you can work on fine details like hair strands and blurred background edges, achieving transitions that low `Opacity` simply cannot match.

    Think of pencil shading: using an HB pencil, you gently apply layer after layer to gradually darken the tone. Flow works exactly the same way — gentle, repeated applications produce smooth, cumulative results.

#### 12.3 A More Intuitive Stylus Experience

When you map stylus pressure to control `Flow` (with `Opacity` pressure sensitivity turned off), your stroke pressure directly controls the speed of "hiding" or "revealing."

- **Light touch**: Flow is extremely low, barely perceptible change — perfect for micro-adjustments on an already good transition.

    A very light touch produces an almost invisible stroke, allowing you to make extremely fine adjustments.

- **Heavy pressure**: Flow becomes high, allowing you to quickly hide or reveal large areas.

    Heavy pressure covers large areas rapidly.

This sense of unity between hand and brush is the foundation of advanced masking technique.

### 12.4 Mask Operation: Best Practice Workflow

You can use this directly as the "Golden Rules for Mask Brush Settings" in your teaching material.

1. **Select the mask**: In the Layers Panel, make sure you click the layer's mask thumbnail (it will have a white border).

    Confirm the mask is selected — the thumbnail should have a white border around it.

2. **Select the Brush Tool (B)**: Typically use a **soft round brush**.

    A soft brush produces soft edges, ideal for creating smooth transitions.

3. **Set the core parameters**:

    - **Opacity**: `100%`

        Lock Opacity at 100%.

    - **Flow**: Start between `5%` and `30%`.

        Start with a low Flow and adjust as needed.

        - `~5%` for extremely fine edges, such as hair strands, glow effects.

            The finest work uses the lowest Flow.

        - `~20%` for most regular edge transitions.

            Regular operations use medium Flow.

        - `~50%` or higher for quickly hiding/revealing large areas.

            Larger area operations can increase Flow.

4. **Set up the stylus (if using one)**:

    - Press `F5` to open the Brush Settings panel.

    - Go to **Transfer** or the **Opacity/Flow Jitter** options.

    - Ensure the `Control` for **Opacity Jitter** is set to **Off**.

        Turn off Opacity pressure sensitivity.

    - Set the `Control` for **Flow Jitter** to **Pen Pressure**.

        Enable Flow pressure sensitivity for hand-brush unity.

5. **Begin operation**:

    - Paint on the mask with **black** to **hide** the current layer's content.

        Black = Hide.

    - Paint on the mask with **white** to **reveal** the current layer's content.

        White = Reveal.

    - **Technique**: Leverage the cumulative property of low Flow — gently brush back and forth over edge areas that need smooth transitions until the effect is perfect.

### 12.5 Are There Exceptions? When Can Opacity Be Used in Masks?

Yes, but the scenarios are very limited.

When you need a **quick, uniform, precise** semi-transparent effect — not a gradual transition — you can use Opacity.

- **Example**: You want a layer to appear 50% semi-transparent overall. Instead of adjusting the layer's own Opacity, you can add a mask, then use a large brush at 50% Opacity (Flow 100%) to **click once**, giving the entire mask a uniform 50% gray. Alternatively, you can fill the mask with 50% gray directly (Shift+F5).

    This scenario is rare: when you need a simple semi-transparent effect with no transition at all.

But whenever **painting** and **transitions** are involved, the `100% Opacity + Low Flow` combination is always the more professional, higher-quality choice.

### 12.6 Summary

| Parameter | Recommended Setting | Core Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Opacity** | **100%** | Avoids the "stamping effect," provides a stable ceiling for Flow, making results more controllable |
| **Flow** | **5% – 30% (adjust as needed)** | Enables smooth, organic color accumulation, creating natural transitions — the key to precise work |
| **Stylus pressure** | Controls Flow | Provides the most intuitive control, closest to the feel of traditional drawing — achieving hand-brush unity |
